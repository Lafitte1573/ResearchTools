# Automatic Data Visualization Generation from Chinese Natural Language Questions

## Core Ideas
This paper addresses the significant gap in **Text-to-Vis (Text-to-Visualization)** research regarding the Chinese language. While automatic generation of data visualizations from natural language (NL) questions has gained traction in English contexts, there is a lack of datasets and methodologies for Chinese users. The authors identify two primary challenges in Chinese Text-to-Vis:
1.  **Cross-Lingual Mismatch:** In real-world scenarios, database schemas (table and column names) are often in English (due to programming conventions), while user queries are in Chinese. This requires the model to possess strong cross-lingual alignment capabilities.
2.  **Word Segmentation Ambiguity:** Chinese text lacks explicit word boundaries. Standard tokenization (e.g., treating every character as a token) or erroneous segmentation can prevent the model from correctly linking NL terms to database schema elements.

To tackle these issues, the paper presents **CNvBench**, the first large-scale Chinese Text-to-Vis dataset, and proposes a baseline model that integrates **Multilingual BERT** with **n-gram information** to enhance semantic encoding and schema linking.

## Key Innovations
*   **CNvBench Dataset:** The authors introduce the first large-scale benchmark for Chinese Text-to-Vis. It is constructed by translating the English NvBench dataset while intentionally retaining English database schemas. This design simulates real-world conditions where Chinese users query English-structured databases, creating a necessary cross-lingual learning environment.
*   **N-gram Enhanced Encoding:** To mitigate Chinese word segmentation errors, the model injects **n-gram representations** into the encoder. Unlike standard BERT tokenization which might split meaningful Chinese words, the n-gram encoder captures multi-character semantic units (e.g., "散点图" for scatter plot) and fuses them with character-level embeddings.
*   **Cross-Lingual Baseline Model (BRIDGE_MN):** The paper adapts the state-of-the-art BRIDGE model for the Chinese context. It replaces the original encoder with a **Multilingual BERT** to handle the Chinese-English schema mismatch and adds a specialized n-gram injection mechanism to improve Chinese semantic understanding.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The study provides extensive experiments comparing different encoding strategies (LSTM vs. BERT) and segmentation tools, establishing a strong baseline for future research in non-English Text-to-Vis tasks.

## Method & Implementation

### 1. Dataset Construction (CNvBench)
*   **Source:** Derived from **NvBench** (Luo et al., 2021), a large-scale English Text-to-Vis dataset containing 25,750 NL-Vis pairs.
*   **Translation Process:**
    *   Questions were translated from English to Chinese by NLP researchers and students.
    *   **Crucial Design Choice:** Database schemas (table names, column names, values) were **kept in English**. This forces the model to learn cross-lingual alignment rather than simple string matching.
    *   Translation quality was ensured through a multi-step review process (translate -> review -> revise -> compare).
*   **Structure:** The dataset maintains the four hardness levels of NvBench (Easy, Medium, Hard, Extra Hard) based on the complexity of the required visualization logic.

### 2. Model Architecture
The proposed model, denoted as **BRIDGE_MN**, follows an Encoder-Decoder structure tailored for cross-lingual Text-to-Vis.

#### A. Encoder: Multilingual BERT + N-gram Injection
The encoder is designed to solve the language mismatch and segmentation issues.
*   **Multilingual BERT:** Serves as the base encoder to map Chinese questions and English schema items into a shared semantic space.
*   **N-gram Encoder:**
    *   **Motivation:** Standard WordPiece tokenization in BERT treats Chinese characters individually or in sub-words, potentially missing whole-word semantics (e.g., splitting "visualization" concepts).
    *   **Mechanism:**
        1.  **Extraction:** Extracts n-grams (sequences of characters) from the input Chinese question.
        2.  **Encoding:** A multi-layer Transformer encodes these n-grams into vector representations.
        3.  **Injection:** The n-gram representations are injected into the original BERT encoder layers. For each character, its representation is combined with the representations of associated n-grams.
    *   **Benefit:** This enriches the character embeddings with contextual word-level information, helping the model recognize database columns even if segmentation is imperfect.

#### B. Decoder: LSTM-based Pointer-Generator
*   **Base:** Inherited from the BRIDGE model (Lin et al., 2020).
*   **Function:** Generates the visualization specification in **Vega-Lite** (a declarative visualization language).
*   **Pointer-Generator Network:**
    *   At each decoding step, the decoder can either generate a token from the fixed vocabulary (e.g., Vega-Lite keywords like "mark", "encoding") OR copy a token directly from the input sequence (e.g., specific column names like "customer_id").
    *   This copying mechanism is vital for handling out-of-vocabulary schema items.
*   **Output:** The generated Vega-Lite JSON specification is rendered into a chart using the Vega-Lite engine.

### 3. Implementation Details
*   **Segmentation Tools:** For LSTM baselines, the authors tested two Chinese word segmentation tools: **Jieba** and **HanLP**.
*   **Embeddings:** LSTM models used Tencent multilingual embeddings.
*   **Training:** The model is trained to maximize the likelihood of the ground-truth Vega-Lite specification given the NL question and database schema.

## Experimental Results

### 1. Experimental Setup
*   **Metrics:**
    *   **Vis Tree Matching Accuracy:** Measures if the generated Vega-Lite syntax tree matches the ground truth structure.
    *   **Vis Matching Accuracy:** Measures if the specific visualization components (chart type, axes, data fields) are correct.
*   **Baselines:**
    *   **LSTM:** Standard LSTM encoder with pre-trained word embeddings (tested with Jieba and HanLP segmentation).
    *   **BRIDGE_M:** The proposed model using Multilingual BERT *without* n-gram injection.
    *   **BRIDGE_MN:** The full proposed model (Multilingual BERT + N-gram injection).

### 2. Quantitative Performance
The results demonstrate the superiority of pre-trained transformers and the specific benefit of n-gram injection for Chinese.

| Model | Easy | Medium | Hard | Extra Hard | **All (Overall)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **LSTM** | 0.527 | 0.534 | 0.479 | 0.486 | **0.501** |
| **BRIDGE_M** | 0.833 | 0.824 | 0.751 | 0.760 | **0.804** |
| **BRIDGE_MN** | **0.836** | **0.842** | **0.776** | **0.801** | **0.812** |

*   **Pre-trained Models vs. LSTM:** BRIDGE models significantly outperform LSTM baselines (~80% vs ~50% accuracy). This highlights that modeling context and cross-lingual relationships requires the deep semantic understanding provided by Transformers.
*   **Impact of N-grams:** **BRIDGE_MN** outperforms **BRIDGE_M** by approximately **1%** overall (0.812 vs 0.804). While seemingly small, this improvement is consistent across all hardness levels, confirming that n-gram injection helps resolve segmentation ambiguities in Chinese.
*   **Hardness Levels:** Performance drops as query complexity increases (from Easy to Extra Hard), indicating that complex logical reasoning in Chinese Text-to-Vis remains a challenge.

### 3. Segmentation Analysis
*   The authors compared LSTM performance using **Jieba** ($LSTM_J$) vs. **HanLP** ($LSTM_H$).
*   Results showed slight variations, indicating that LSTM-based models are sensitive to the quality of external segmentation tools.
*   In contrast, BERT-based models (BRIDGE) are less dependent on external segmentation due to their sub-word tokenization and the internal n-gram enhancement.

### 4. Conclusion on Results
The experiments validate that:
1.  **CNvBench is Challenging:** Even state-of-the-art models struggle with "Extra Hard" queries (80% accuracy), leaving room for future improvement.
2.  **Cross-Lingual Ability is Key:** The success of Multilingual BERT confirms its ability to align Chinese questions with English schemas.
3.  **N-grams Help:** Incorporating n-gram information provides a measurable boost in semantic parsing accuracy for Chinese inputs.

### 5. Limitations
*   **Dataset Split:** The study focuses on a question-based split. The authors note that semantic parsing datasets often require consideration of database splits or template splits to fully evaluate generalization, which was not the primary focus here.
*   **Translation Artifacts:** As the dataset is translated, there may be subtle nuances lost compared to natively collected Chinese queries, though rigorous review was conducted to minimize this.
