# CHARTHAL: A Fine-grained Framework Evaluating Hallucination of Large Vision Language Models in Chart Understanding

## Core Ideas
The paper addresses a critical gap in the evaluation of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMS) for chart understanding: while prior work has focused on overall accuracy or object/scene hallucination, it has largely neglected *chart-specific* hallucinations—errors where models fabricate information that is either non-existent in the chart or inconsistent with its visual data. The core problem is that chart understanding demands both high perceptual fidelity and rigorous factual accuracy; hallucinations here are especially harmful because they can mislead users in data-driven decision-making.

The authors introduce **CHARTHAL**, the first fine-grained benchmark for evaluating hallucinations in LVLMS on chart question-answering tasks. Its key conceptual contributions are:
- A novel, human-validated taxonomy of 12 hallucination-triggering scenarios, organized along two independent dimensions: **Question Type** (descriptive, reasoning, open-ended) and **Chart-Question Relation** (irrelevant, nonexistent, contradictory, normal).
- A human-validated dataset of 1,062 samples spanning diverse chart types and academic disciplines.
- A binary scoring system to quantify hallucination severity per response, enabling precise diagnosis rather than just final answer correctness.

The paper demonstrates that even state-of-the-art proprietary models like GPT-5 and o4-mini achieve only 34.46% and 22.79% accuracy on CHARTHAL, respectively, with errors concentrated in unanswerable cases involving nonexistent or contradictory information—highlighting a severe vulnerability in current LVLMS.

## Key Innovations
1. **Fine-grained Taxonomy of Hallucination Scenarios**: Unlike prior benchmarks that treat hallucination as a binary outcome (correct/incorrect), CHARTHAL classifies errors into 12 distinct categories based on *why* and *how* a model fails. This is achieved by combining two orthogonal axes: 
   - *Question Type*: Descriptive (direct extraction), Reasoning (inference/logic), Open-ended (analytical/predictive).
   - *Chart-Question Relation*: Irrelevant (no chart content needed), Inexistent (asks for missing info), Contradictory (false premise), Normal (valid query).

2. **Human-Validated Dataset & Annotation Protocol**: The dataset is constructed through a three-stage pipeline (collection → QA pair generation → human verification) and includes expert annotation to ensure quality and ground truth validity. This provides a reliable foundation for diagnosing model behavior.

3. **Binary Hallucination Scoring System**: Instead of relying solely on whether the final answer is correct, CHARTHAL uses a binary score per response: 1 if any hallucination occurs (i.e., fabricated or inconsistent information), 0 otherwise. This allows for granular analysis of *where* and *when* hallucinations happen.

4. **Systematic Analysis of Model Behavior**: The framework enables deep investigation into how different model families (proprietary vs. open-source), sizes, and prompting strategies affect hallucination rates across specific scenario types—revealing patterns such as “larger models perform better overall but still fail catastrophically on contradictory questions.”

5. **Case Study Visualization**: Figure 2 provides illustrative examples showing exactly which parts of a model’s response are correct (green) versus hallucinated (red), offering intuitive insight into failure modes.

## Method & Implementation
### Benchmark Design (Section 3.1)
- **Task Formulation**: Free-form chart question answering — models generate natural language answers without multiple-choice constraints, reflecting real-world usage more faithfully than Yes/No or MCQ formats.
- **Taxonomy Construction**: Based on two independent dimensions:
  - *Question Type*: 
    - *Descriptive*: Directly extractable from chart (e.g., “What is the value at x=0.5?”).
    - *Reasoning*: Require computation/logical inference (e.g., “By how much does Y increase when X goes from 0.1 to 0.3?”).
    - *Open-ended*: Analytical or predictive (e.g., “How could we interpret the trend?”).
  - *Chart-Question Relation*:
    - *Irrelevant*: Question unrelated to chart content.
    - *Inexistent*: Asks about information not present in chart (e.g., “What was the average monthly Twitter follower growth over the period?” when no time axis exists).
    - *Contradictory*: Premise contradicts chart data (e.g., “The highest W_L value occurred in 2020” when chart shows it occurred in 2021).
    - *Normal*: Valid question with consistent premises.

This yields 3 × 4 = 12 categories, summarized in Table 1.

### Dataset Construction (Section 3.2)
A three-stage pipeline:
1. **Image Collection**: Sampled from CharXiv, covering scientific charts from arXiv papers across disciplines (e.g., ML, physics, biology).
2. **QA Pair Generation**: Using o4-mini to generate questions and corresponding ground-truth answers for each chart, ensuring coverage across all 12 subcategories.
3. **Human Verification**: Expert annotators refine answers and verify correctness, ensuring high-quality labels.

Total: 1,062 high-quality samples, distributed across 12 categories as shown in Table 1.

### Evaluation Criteria (Section 3.3)
- **Binary Scoring**: Each model response receives a score of 1 if it contains *any* hallucination (fabricated or inconsistent information), 0 otherwise.
- **Normalization**: Scores are normalized to [0,100] for readability.
- **Metrics**: Overall score = average of binary scores across all samples; also reported per category and model.

### Experimental Setup (Section 4.1)
- **Models Tested**: 15 SOTA LVLMS:
  - *Proprietary*: Gemini-2.5-Pro, GPT-5 series, GPT-5-mini, GPT-5-nano, o4-mini, GPT-4o-2024-11-20.
  - *Open-source*: Llama-3.2-11B-Vision-Instruct, InternVL2.5 series, Qwen2.5-VL series.
- **Evaluation Protocol**: Responses scored using criteria defined in Section 3.3; detailed settings in Appendix B.

## Experimental Results
### Main Results (Table 2, Section 4.2)
- **Overall Performance**: 
  - Best performer: **Qwen2.5-VL-72B** with **54.24%** overall score (best and second-best marked bold/underlined).
  - Worst performers: **o4-mini** (22.79%) and **GPT-5-mini (high)** (27.02%).
  - Proprietary models lag significantly: GPT-5 (34.37%), o4-mini (22.79%).

- **Per Category Breakdown**:
  - *Descriptive*: Highest scores (~60% for top models), indicating strong extraction capability.
  - *Reasoning*: Moderate scores (~50% for top models), suggesting difficulty with inference.
  - *Open-ended*: Lowest scores (<40% for most models), highlighting poor analytical/predictive abilities.

  - *Irrelevant*: ~36–50% for top models.
  - *Inexistent*: ~29–69% — notably low for smaller models (e.g., o4-mini: 10.78%).
  - *Contradictory*: Very low for many models — e.g., o4-mini: 1.90%, Qwen2.5-VL-72B: 19.05%.
  - *Normal*: Highest scores (~65–75%), confirming models handle valid queries well.

- **Key Observations**:
  - **Unanswerable Cases Are Most Problematic**: Only 14.53% of *inexistent* questions are correctly recognized as unanswerable by Qwen2.5-VL-72B, versus 69.77% for *normal* ones.
  - **Accuracy Drops on Contradictory Questions**: Accuracy drops by 3.33% compared to normal questions.
  - **Model Size Matters**: Larger models (Qwen2.5-VL-72B, Gemini-2.5-Pro) outperform smaller ones, though not always proportionally.
  - **Training Bias**: Many models show bias toward generating answers regardless of answerability, leading to hallucinations in unanswerable cases.

### Qualitative Analysis (Figure 2, Section 5)
- **Question Type Analysis**: Descriptive questions yield >60% scores for top models; reasoning questions moderate (~50%); open-ended questions worst (<45%).
- **Chart-Question Relation Analysis**: Models perform best on *normal* questions, struggle heavily on *contradictory* and *inexistent* ones. For example, in one case, GPT-5 incorrectly states “the sampling frequency can be calculated…” despite the chart lacking this info.
- **Model Size Impact**: Larger models generally do better, but smaller ones occasionally outperform them in specific subcategories (e.g., GPT-5 vs. GPT-5-mini on some reasoning tasks).
- **High Reasoning Impact**: Increasing reasoning effort yields minimal gains and may even hurt performance due to misinterpretation amplifying errors.

## Limitations
The paper acknowledges several important limitations:

1. **Limited Dataset Scope**: The dataset is built primarily from scientific charts sampled from CharXiv, which limits generalizability to domains like business, journalism, or education where chart styles and conventions differ significantly.

2. **Insufficient Taxonomy Coverage**: While the taxonomy covers many empirically observed hallucination triggers, it may not encompass all real-world scenarios—especially context-dependent, adversarial, or highly domain-specific questions.

3. **Lack of Mitigation Evaluation**: The work focuses on *diagnosing* hallucinations but does not evaluate mitigation techniques (e.g., adversarial training, calibration, abstention). Future work could integrate these methods and compare their effectiveness.

4. **Human Annotation Reliance**: The high-quality dataset depends on expert annotators, which introduces potential subjectivity and scalability challenges for larger-scale deployment.

5. **Static Evaluation**: The benchmark evaluates static responses without considering iterative interaction or multi-turn dialogue, which may be necessary for complex chart exploration.

## Conclusion
CHARTHAL represents a significant step forward in evaluating the reliability of LVLMS for chart understanding. By introducing a fine-grained taxonomy and a human-validated dataset, it enables systematic diagnosis of hallucination behaviors—not just final accuracy. The results reveal critical vulnerabilities: even advanced models like GPT-5 and o4-mini suffer severe hallucinations, particularly in unanswerable cases involving nonexistent or contradictory information. This underscores the urgent need for robust mitigation strategies such as adversarial training, calibration, or abstention mechanisms to make LVLMS trustworthy in data-critical applications.

The paper concludes by emphasizing that while LVLMS handle standard chart questions reasonably well, they remain highly vulnerable to hallucination in unanswerable scenarios—a finding that calls for deeper research into improving model trustworthiness in visual reasoning tasks.
